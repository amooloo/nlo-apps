Post-Op: Braces — General Instructions
For new brace placements and routine adjustments.

Patient Information

Instructions

Review with Patient

Clinical Reminders

  1. Soreness: Normal 3–5 days. Ibuprofen or acetaminophen as directed. Warm saltwater rinses (½ tsp / 8 oz, 3×/day).
  2. Foods to avoid: Hard, crunchy, sticky, chewy. Ice, hard candy, nuts, popcorn, bagels, pizza crust, gum, caramel, taffy. Cut apples and carrots into small pieces.
  3. Oral hygiene: Brush after every meal. Floss nightly with threaders or ortho picks. Fluoride rinse recommended.
  4. Wax: Roll a pea-size piece, dry the area, press over offending bracket or wire.
  5. Poking wire: Press flat with a pencil eraser. If persistent, call to schedule a wire trim.
  6. Loose bracket: Do not remove. Call to schedule a repair.
  7. Elastics: Wear 22+ hrs/day. Remove only to eat and brush.
  8. Sports: Orthodontic mouthguard required for all contact sports.
Post-Op: Bonding / Debond
After bracket bonding or full debond.

Patient Information

Instructions

Review with Patient

Clinical Reminders

  1. Bond day: No eating × 1 hr. No hard, crunchy, or sticky foods × 24 hrs to allow adhesive cure.
  2. Sensitivity: Mild tooth sensitivity is normal 1–2 days post-bonding.
  3. After debond: Teeth may feel slightly rough — resolves within weeks.
  4. White spots: Decalcification after debond requires daily fluoride rinse and GP follow-up.
  5. Retainers: Begin wearing immediately. Do not sleep without retainer the first night.
  6. Post-debond sensitivity: Teeth may feel loose or sensitive for 2–4 weeks — normal as PDL adapts.

RPE or Schwarz appliance instructions.

Instructions

Review with Patient

Clinical Reminders

  1. Activation: Insert key, turn toward back of mouth in direction of arrow. One full turn = one activation. Do NOT double-turn unless instructed.

Post-Op: Functional Appliance — Framework Delivery
For MARA, Herbst, and similar fixed Class II appliances. Given at the cementation visit (Visit 2). Framework only — arms / elbows / pistons are NOT connected today.

Patient Information

Framework-Only Instructions

Review with Patient
What was done today: The upper and lower components of your appliance were cemented to your back teeth. The connecting arms / elbows / pistons are NOT attached yet — that happens at your next visit (~1 month). For the next 4 weeks, you will wear the framework alone to get used to it. Your jaw will NOT be advanced during this acclimation period.

Acclimation Phase (Weeks 1–4)

  1. Soreness: Cheek, lip, tongue, and tooth tenderness expected for 3–5 days. Ibuprofen 200–400 mg q6–8h with food OR acetaminophen 500–650 mg q6h. Warm saltwater rinses (½ tsp / 8 oz) 3–4×/day.
  2. Wax for irritation: Roll a pea-size piece, dry the area, press over rubbing component. Rinse with saltwater after meals.
  3. Speech: A temporary lisp or muffled speech is common for 1–2 weeks. Read aloud daily — your tongue will adapt.
  4. Eating — first 3–5 days: Soft foods only — pasta, eggs, yogurt, smoothies, mashed potatoes, soup. Cut everything small.
  5. Permanent diet rules: No hard, crunchy, sticky, or chewy foods for the duration of treatment. No ice, gum, caramel, taffy, hard candy, popcorn, nuts, hard bread, or whole apples / carrots. These will dislodge or break the appliance.
  6. Hygiene is critical: Brush 2×/day with care around bands, arms, and welds. Use a water flosser daily. Fluoride rinse nightly. Plaque under bands causes decay.
  7. RPE turning (if applicable): If your appliance includes an expander (RPE), begin home turning tonight — 1 turn per night before bed. Total turns: 10–14 (MARA) or up to 21 (Herbst), as instructed by Dr. Akhavan. A midline gap between the upper front teeth is expected and is a good sign. Do NOT exceed the prescribed total.
  8. Sports / mouthguards: Use a soft orthodontic mouthguard for contact sports.
  9. What is NOT happening yet: Your jaw is not being repositioned during this acclimation phase. The "Class II correction" begins at your next visit when the arms / elbows / pistons are connected.
  10. Loose / broken appliance: Do NOT pull, twist, or remove. Stop activation if turning. Take a photo and call the office same day.
  11. Sharp poke / tissue cut: Cover with wax immediately. If unable to relieve, call for a same-day adjustment.
  12. Call the office if: Severe pain not relieved by OTC after 48 hrs · unable to close mouth · inability to eat after 5 days · loose component · fever > 101°F or pus.
  13. Next visit (~1 month): Dr. Akhavan will connect the arms / elbows / pistons. You will receive a new instruction sheet at that visit covering the activation phase, including the limited-opening rule that becomes important once the arms are attached.
Post-Op: Functional Appliance — Activation
For MARA, Herbst, and similar fixed Class II appliances. Given at the activation visit (~1 month after framework delivery). Arms / elbows / pistons are now connected — jaw posturing begins.

Patient Information

Activation Phase Instructions

Review with Patient
What changed today: The connecting arms / elbows / pistons are now attached. Your lower jaw is being held in a forward-postured position — this is when the actual Class II correction begins. Your bite will feel different. This is the appliance working.

Living With an Activated Functional Appliance

  1. Bite changes are normal: Your bite will feel "off" the first 1–2 weeks as your jaw adapts to the forward posture. Some pressure on the front teeth and TMJ area is expected.
  2. LIMITED OPENING RULE (CRITICAL): Do NOT open your mouth wide. The arms / elbows can disengage if you open too far — they may slip out of position or become bent. Practice opening just enough to fit two finger-widths between your front teeth. Yawning, biting into thick sandwiches, or wide laughter are common ways the arms slip out.
  3. If an arm slips out: Do NOT try to push it back in yourself. Cover any sharp end with wax and call the office for a same-day or next-day adjustment.
  4. Soreness from activation: Mild jaw, tooth, and TMJ soreness for 3–5 days after each activation visit. Ibuprofen or acetaminophen as before. Warm saltwater rinses help.
  5. Speech: Speech changes again briefly when arms are first attached — clears in days to a week. Read aloud.
  6. Eating: Continue all the food restrictions from the framework phase — no hard, crunchy, sticky, or chewy foods. Cut food into small pieces. Avoid biting forcefully into anything.
  7. Hygiene: Continue thorough brushing 2×/day around bands, arms, and welds. Water flosser is essential. Fluoride rinse nightly.
  8. Wax for irritation: Cheek or lip rubbing is common when arms are first attached. Wax over offending components. Saltwater rinses 3–4×/day to keep tissue healthy. Persistent ulcers — call the office.
  9. Activation visits: Routine activation visits are every ~3 months. Dr. Akhavan adds shims at each visit to progressively advance the jaw further. Do not skip these.
  10. Total treatment time: 9–12 months total (longer in some cases — up to 18 months for Class II Division 2 or deep bite cases).
  11. Sports: Continue using a soft orthodontic mouthguard for contact sports.
  12. Call the office if: An arm or piston slips out · severe pain unrelieved by OTC after 48 hrs · cannot close your mouth · ulcer or sore that does not heal · loose band or component · fever > 101°F or pus.
Post-Op: MSE Delivery & Activation
For Maxillary Skeletal Expander — TADs placed in-office with the appliance as a surgical guide.

Patient Information

Instructions

Review with Patient
About MSE: The MSE is a palatal expander anchored to your upper teeth and to two miniscrews (TADs) placed in your palate today. The appliance and TADs work together to widen your upper jaw skeletally. A midline gap (diastema) between the upper front teeth is expected and is a positive sign that the suture is opening — it closes naturally during the consolidation phase.

Clinical Reminders

  1. First 24–48 hours after TAD placement: Mild palatal soreness and pressure are expected. Ice externally (20 min on / off) for the first 24 hrs if needed. OTC pain relief (ibuprofen 200–400 mg q6–8h with food, or acetaminophen 500–650 mg q6h) is sufficient. No food or hot liquids for 1 hour after placement.
  2. Activation — turning the key: 1 turn per day using the key provided (each full turn = 0.25 mm of expansion). Best done before bed with the patient lying back; a parent or partner should assist. Insert the key into the activation hole and push toward the back of the mouth until the next hole is visible. Remove carefully. Do NOT double-turn unless specifically instructed by Dr. Akhavan.
  3. Midline diastema is normal: A gap will appear between the upper front teeth as the suture opens — this is a good sign. The diastema closes naturally during the consolidation phase; do not try to close it artificially.
  4. Pressure & sensation: Mild pressure or tingling in the face, nose, or front teeth after each turn is normal for the first few days. Reduces over time.
  5. TAD care & oral hygiene: Brush gently around the appliance and TAD heads twice daily with a soft toothbrush. Use a water flosser or proxy brush to clear debris around the TADs. Chlorhexidine rinse (0.12%) 2×/day for the first 2 weeks if prescribed.
  6. Diet: Soft foods for the first 1–2 days. Avoid hard, sticky, or crunchy foods throughout treatment — they can dislodge the appliance.
  7. Speech: A temporary lisp is normal for 1–2 weeks. Reading aloud helps the tongue adapt.
  8. Stop turning & call the office immediately if: a TAD feels loose · the appliance shifts or comes off a tooth · severe / persistent pain not relieved by OTC · nosebleeds that do not stop · the appliance hits the lower teeth when biting.
  9. Consolidation phase: Once the goal expansion is reached, activation stops but the appliance stays in for 6–9 months while the bone fills the expanded suture. Do not skip follow-up appointments during this phase — the appliance must remain stable until removal.
Post-Op: MARPE Activation
For Mini-Implant Assisted Rapid Palatal Expander — TADs placed by oral surgeon / periodontist; NLO handles activation only.

Patient Information

Activation Instructions

Review with Patient
About your MARPE: Your TADs were placed by your oral surgeon / periodontist. Today we performed the initial in-office activation of the appliance. The MARPE jackscrew (Tiger Screw / H-Screw) is different from a standard expander — each "swing" of the screw advances 1/12 mm. Your job at home is to continue activating on schedule until the goal is reached.

Tiger Screw / H-Screw Home Activation Protocol

  1. Schedule: 1–2 swings per day at home, every day, starting today. Do NOT skip days. Each swing = 1/12 mm. 12 swings = 1 mm. 6 swings = 0.5 mm.
  2. Direction matters: The hex screw has a direction arrow — turn ONLY in the direction of the arrow. Use the dotted-surface pattern on the screw (1 dot, blank, 2 dots, blank, 3 dots, blank, then repeats) to confirm you are turning correctly. Wrong-direction turning becomes immediately obvious — if you see an unexpected pattern, stop and call the office.
  3. Use the correct wrench: Use only the Tiger Screw / H-Screw wrench provided — NOT a standard expander wrench.
  4. Tracking: Use the patient tracking chart provided. Each visible surface of the hex = 1 mm of expansion. Mark each surface as it becomes visible. 36 boxes = 6 mm = the manufacturer maximum.
  5. Visible scale rings: Etched rings on the large arm of the appliance — each visible ring = 2 mm of expansion. Use these as an independent visual check on your progress at every appointment.
  6. Midline diastema (the gap): A gap between the upper front teeth should appear within the first 2 weeks of home activation — this is a good sign that the palate is opening. The gap closes naturally later; do not try to close it.
  7. 2-WEEK STOP RULE: If after 2 weeks of home activation NO midline diastema has appeared, STOP TURNING and call the office. Continuing to activate when the suture is not opening can damage the appliance or your TADs.
  8. Pressure & sensation: Mild pressure in the palate, face, nose, and front teeth after each swing is normal. OTC pain relief if needed.
  9. Hygiene around the appliance: Brush gently around the appliance and TAD sites 2×/day with a soft toothbrush. Use a water flosser daily. Chlorhexidine rinse 2×/day for the first 2 weeks.
  10. Diet: No hard, sticky, or crunchy foods for the duration of treatment. They can dislodge the appliance.
  11. Stop activating & call the office immediately if: a TAD becomes mobile · the appliance comes off a tooth · severe / persistent pain · the screw stops turning or feels jammed · no diastema after 2 weeks of home turning · the wrench feels different (could be wrong-direction turning).
  12. Endpoint: The manufacturer maximum is 6 mm (36 swings of expansion). Dr. Akhavan will tell you when to stop based on your specific case — the visible warning threads on the screw are the absolute hard stop.
  13. Follow-up cadence: Follow-ups are progressively spaced — 1 week after starting, then 2 weeks, then 3 weeks, then standard cadence. Closer early supervision is intentional; do not skip the early visits.

Post-Op Instructions

Review with Patient
Electrosurgery Note: Treated tissue will appear white, gray, or have a thin surface crust (eschar) for several days. This is normal — do NOT remove it. It sloughs naturally within 3–7 days.

Clinical Reminders — Review Before Printing

  1. Bleeding: Minor oozing normal × 2–4 hrs. Firm gauze pressure 10–15 min if needed. Moistened tea bag helps. No straws, forceful spitting, or aggressive rinsing × 24 hrs. Bleeding > 30 min that does not respond to pressure → call office.
  2. Pain: Ibuprofen 400–600 mg q6–8h with food OR acetaminophen 500–650 mg q6h (max 3 g / day). No aspirin. Ice (20 min on / off) × 24 hrs. Mild burning from electrosurgery for 1–2 days is expected.
  3. Swelling: Peaks 48–72 hrs. Elevate head. Ice × 24 hrs then warm compress. Resolves end of week 1.
  4. Hygiene: No brushing or flossing at site × 24 hrs. After 24 hrs: warm saltwater (½ tsp / 8 oz) 2–3×/day. Chlorhexidine if prescribed. Soft brush of surrounding teeth from day 2.
  5. Diet: Soft, cool-to-lukewarm × 3–5 days. No hard, crunchy, very hot, spicy, or acidic foods. No straws × 48 hrs.
  6. Activity: No strenuous exercise × 24–48 hrs. No smoking or tobacco × 72 hrs. No alcohol × 24 hrs or while on Rx.
  7. Medications: Complete full antibiotic course if prescribed.
  8. Healing timeline: Eschar sloughs days 3–7. Sensitivity 1–2 wks. Tissue remodeling 4–8 wks. Final result 3 mos.
  9. Call immediately if: Bleeding > 30 min unrelieved by pressure · fever > 101°F · pus or increasing swelling · difficulty swallowing or breathing · allergic reaction.
Aligner Instructions
For NLO Next Level Aligner, Invisalign, Angel Aligners, and Oliv patients.

Patient Information

Instructions

Review with Patient

Clinical Reminders

  1. Wear time: 22+ hrs/day. Remove only to eat, drink (except plain water), and brush/floss.
  2. Changing trays: On schedule, at night so soreness passes during sleep. Keep ALL previous trays.
  3. Attachments: Tooth-colored bumps are normal and necessary. Do not pick at them.
  4. Cleaning: Cool water rinse on removal. Gentle toothbrush — no toothpaste. Retainer Brite weekly.
  5. Hygiene: Brush AND floss before reinserting after meals. Food + aligner = cavities.
  6. No hot liquids: Warps the plastic. Room-temp water only with aligners in.
  7. Soreness: Normal 1–2 days each new tray. OTC pain relief.
  8. Lost or cracked tray: Call immediately. Wear previous tray. Do NOT skip ahead.
Retainer Instructions

Patient Information

Instructions

Review with Patient

Clinical Reminders

  1. Wear as directed: Full-time × 3–6 months, then nights only forever. Teeth WILL shift without retainer.

Clinician Reminders — Review Before Signing

  1. Root resorption: Orthodontic forces can shorten root length. Typically minor. Radiographic monitoring.
  2. Decalcification / cavities: Plaque around brackets causes permanent white spots. Preventable with hygiene.
  3. Periodontal disease: Must maintain gum health. Poor hygiene can necessitate stopping treatment.

  8. Allergies: Patient/RP has disclosed all known allergies to metal, latex, or adhesives.
  9. No guarantees: Ortho aims to improve alignment and function. Results vary by individual.
  10. Right to withdraw: Patient may discontinue, but risks of incomplete treatment have been explained.

Informed Consent — Clear Aligner Treatment
For NLO Next Level Aligner, Invisalign, Angel Aligners, and Oliv patients.

Patient Information

Compliance Agreement & Risks

Review Verbally

Clear Aligner Compliance — Patient Acknowledgments

  1. Wear time: Aligners must be worn ~20–22 hours/day, except to eat, drink, brush, and floss. Less wear time may result in prolonged treatment, non-tracking, suboptimal results, and instability of dental alignment and occlusion.
  2. Attachments: Small tooth-colored attachments and/or buttons will be bonded to many or even all teeth — including front teeth — to help move them properly. Attachments are usually placed on the first visit and may be added or removed later in treatment. They are an essential part of successful aligner treatment for most patients. They are discreet but not invisible. Refusing attachment placement on certain teeth WILL compromise final results. If treatment extends beyond the estimated time due to declining recommended attachments, additional fees may be incurred.
  3. Interproximal reduction (IPR): Reproximation / recontouring / enamel slenderizing — removal of 0.1–0.5 mm of enamel at dental contact points with a fine hand file, disk, or bur. Sometimes required to resolve crowding, facilitate tooth movement, or achieve desired bite changes. Not harmful to teeth and usually quick and painless. Declining IPR when recommended will lead to compromised results.
  4. Elastics: Elastics or "rubber bands" worn from upper to lower teeth may be prescribed to improve the bite. For optimal results they must be worn whenever aligners are in (~20 hrs/day) as instructed.
  5. Aligner care: Always store aligners in their case when not being worn to avoid loss. Repeated loss of multiple aligners may incur a per-aligner replacement fee. Always keep your three most recent previous aligners.
  6. Speech: Most patients normalize speech after the first week of aligner wear. There is an acclimation period during which aligners must be worn constantly despite a slight speech impediment so the tongue can retrain for proper articulation.
  7. Chewies: Use chewies / aligner seaters daily to keep treatment on track and ensure aligners seat fully on the teeth.
  8. Cavity / staining risk: Eating and drinking with aligners in can cause enamel decalcification and cavities. Remove aligners to eat and drink anything other than flat water; brush or rinse with water before re-inserting. Failing to do so will cause cavities and staining of aligners, attachments, and teeth.
  9. Appointments: Keep your scheduled appointments — this is the only way to ensure treatment is progressing as planned. Always wear your current aligner to your appointment.
  10. Aligner batches & refinements: Aligners are made and given in batches. After the first batch, additional "refinement" aligners (possibly including fixed braces or settling elastics) may be necessary to detail the smile and bite. The treatment fee includes a defined number of batches / refinements; tooth movement beyond what can be achieved within those rounds may incur additional fees per the financial agreement.
  11. Possible need for traditional braces: Some tooth movements are not efficiently or predictably achieved with clear aligners. During treatment, a short course of fixed appliance therapy with elastics plus an occlusal adjustment may be offered to perfect tooth positions and bite. The patient may choose to proceed with a few months of braces or leave teeth in the best position the aligners achieved. If the switch to braces is due to poor compliance with required wear time, a fee may be incurred to cover materials and staff time.
  12. Gingival recession & grafting: Gingival recession and the potential need for a gingival graft by a periodontist is a risk of all orthodontic treatment, but may be elevated with aligner wear, particularly in adults with thin gum tissues. Notify the office if you start to notice developing or worsening gum recession.
  13. Retention: At the end of orthodontic treatment, retainers must be worn as prescribed to maintain the new smile. Retention is for life — or as long as you want to keep your teeth in alignment. (See separate Retention & Relapse consent.)
  14. General orthodontic risks: Same general risks as traditional braces — root resorption, decalcification, TMJ awareness, and relapse. Inadequate wear time remains the leading cause of unsatisfactory outcomes. Results vary by individual; no specific outcome is guaranteed.

Adapted from the American Association of Orthodontists Patient Clear Aligner Supplemental Informed Consent and Compliance Agreement (2024).

Informed Consent — TAD / Mini-Implant
Temporary anchorage device placement.

Patient Information

Required

Risks, Benefits & Limitations

Review Verbally
Purpose of TADs: Temporary anchorage devices provide a fixed point from which to apply orthodontic force to move teeth. They have changed how some malocclusions are treated and may offer advantages over devices like headgear, facemasks, or jaw surgery. TAD use in orthodontic treatment has generally proved safe and predictable, but the outcome cannot be guaranteed.

TAD Risks & Patient Acknowledgments

  1. Tissue irritation: Irritation to oral tissues may result from TAD placement. Generally short-term, resolving without additional treatment once the TAD is removed. Rarely, additional treatment may be necessary. Proper home care as instructed reduces irritation and inflammation.
  2. Local anesthetic: A topical and / or local anesthetic is applied to the gums and / or nearby tissues before placement. Pressure may be felt but not sharp pain. Numbness resolves 1–3 hours. Patient / RP must disclose any prior adverse reaction to anesthetics.
  3. Root proximity / damage: TAD placement is typically in close proximity to teeth. The TAD may come in direct contact with the root of a tooth. The root is rarely harmed; if root damage occurs, it usually self-repairs once the TAD is removed. In rare cases, close monitoring of the root and additional dental procedures may become necessary.
  4. Loosening from forces: Forces placed on teeth and TADs may cause the device to loosen. If the TAD becomes loose it may need to be removed and placed in a different location.
  5. Hypoallergenic material / breakage: TADs are made of medical-grade titanium, a hypoallergenic material. In rare circumstances a TAD may break during insertion or removal. If a residual portion causes irritation, surgical removal may be required.
  6. Tobacco use: Chemicals in tobacco can interfere with bone health after TAD placement and may compromise stability and healing. Tobacco use must be disclosed.
  7. Failure / replacement: Reported failure rates in the literature are approximately 10–20%. Replacement is generally straightforward. Patient must call the office if the TAD becomes loose.
  8. Soreness & aftercare: Soreness expected 3–5 days; OTC pain relief is usually sufficient. Keep clean with chlorhexidine rinse as directed. No food or hot liquids for at least 1 hour post-placement.
  9. Duration & removal: TADs are temporary and are removed when their purpose is complete — typically without anesthesia.
  10. No guarantees: As with any procedure or medical device, the outcome cannot be guaranteed.

Adapted from the American Association of Orthodontists Risks, Benefits and Limitations of TADs (2025).

Informed Consent — Impacted Tooth
For one or more permanent teeth that have not erupted normally and require orthodontic-surgical alignment.

Patient Information

Required

Risks & Acknowledgments

Review Verbally

Impacted Tooth — Patient Acknowledgments

  1. Diagnostic imaging: Additional studies such as 3D / CBCT scans or other special radiographs may be recommended at the outset to determine the exact position of the impacted tooth and surrounding structures.
  2. Imaging during treatment: Additional diagnostic studies may be necessary during orthodontic treatment to assess the position and condition of the involved teeth.
  3. Surgical exposure referral: A referral to an oral surgeon or periodontist may be required to surgically uncover the impacted tooth and / or place a temporary attachment for orthodontic traction. Fees for services performed by other specialists are separate from and additional to NLO orthodontic treatment fees.
  4. Specialist-specific risks: The patient / RP should speak directly with the surgical specialist regarding additional risks and limitations of the surgical procedure.
  5. Detached attachment: A temporary attachment placed on the impacted tooth may detach during orthodontic treatment. The oral surgeon or periodontist may need to replace it, which may require a second surgical procedure.
  6. Ankylosis: In rare cases, an impacted tooth may be fused to the surrounding bone (ankylosis) and may not move with orthodontics. If so, surgical removal of the tooth may be necessary. The space may then be closed with additional orthodontic treatment, or the tooth may need to be replaced by the general dentist (implant, bridge, etc.). The cost of any tooth replacement is separate and additional.
  7. Damage to adjacent teeth and roots: An impacted tooth may have already caused damage to adjacent teeth that is not evident until the tooth is moved. During movement, the impacted tooth may push against adjacent roots and damage them. The root of the impacted tooth itself may also shorten during movement. In severe cases, one or more teeth may be lost due to root shortening. This complication may be unavoidable because of the position of the impacted tooth.
  8. Nerve damage / root canal: During movement of the impacted tooth, the nerve of the impacted tooth or of adjacent teeth may be damaged. This may be unavoidable because of the position of the impacted tooth, and root canal treatment may become necessary. The cost of root canal treatment is separate and additional to the orthodontic fee.
  9. Cosmetic differences: If the impacted tooth is brought into alignment, it may become evident that the tooth is a different shape or color than the adjacent teeth. The patient may choose to ask the general dentist to provide cosmetic correction. The cost of cosmetic correction is separate and additional.
  10. Uneven gum line / recession: When alignment is complete, the gum line along the formerly impacted tooth and adjacent teeth may be uneven. Impacted teeth often experience some gum recession during alignment. Improving the gum line may require services from another dental specialist, the cost of which is separate and additional.
  11. No guarantees: While most impacted teeth can be successfully aligned, the outcome cannot be guaranteed. The risks above may be unavoidable due to the original position of the tooth, and may necessitate a change in the treatment plan including surgical removal of the impacted tooth.

Adapted from the American Association of Orthodontists Informed Consent for an Impacted Tooth or Teeth (Revised 2024).

For in-office electrosurgery gingivectomy and related soft-tissue procedures (troughing, operculectomy, frenectomy).

Patient Information

Required

Risks & Acknowledgments

Review Verbally
About this procedure: A small amount of gum tissue is reshaped or removed using electrosurgery (or laser / scalpel as indicated) to expose more tooth structure, allow proper bracket / band placement, remove an obstructing flap of gum, or release a restrictive frenum. Local anesthetic is used. The procedure is generally safe and well-tolerated, but the outcome cannot be guaranteed.

Patient / Responsible Party Acknowledgments

  1. Local anesthetic: A topical and / or local anesthetic will be applied to the gum tissue before the procedure. Pressure may be felt but not sharp pain. Numbness resolves 1–3 hours. The patient / RP must disclose any prior adverse reaction to anesthetics.
  2. Bleeding: Minor oozing during and immediately after the procedure is normal. Firm gauze pressure controls most bleeding within 10–15 minutes. Persistent bleeding beyond 30 minutes that does not respond to pressure must be reported to the office.
  3. Eschar (tissue crust): With electrosurgery, the treated tissue will appear white, gray, or have a thin surface crust for several days — this is normal and protects the wound. Do NOT scrape or remove the eschar; it sloughs naturally within 3–7 days.
  4. Discomfort: Mild burning, soreness, or pressure for 1–2 days is expected. OTC analgesics (ibuprofen 200–400 mg q6–8h with food, or acetaminophen 500–650 mg q6h, max 3 g / day) are typically sufficient. No aspirin.
  5. Swelling: Mild local swelling may peak at 48–72 hours. Ice externally for the first 24 hours; warm compresses thereafter if needed. Resolves by the end of week 1.
  6. Tissue regrowth: Gum tissue can grow back, particularly if oral hygiene is poor or if the underlying tooth-position issue (e.g., delayed eruption, mouth breathing, hyperplasia) is not addressed. A second procedure may be needed to refine the result.
  7. Cosmetic result & symmetry: Gum-line symmetry and final appearance can vary based on biology and healing. Final tissue contour is not visible until 3 months after the procedure. No specific cosmetic outcome can be guaranteed.
  8. Sensitivity: The exposed tooth surface or root area may be temporarily sensitive to cold, heat, or sweet foods for 1–2 weeks. Sensitivity toothpaste may help.
  9. Adjacent structures: Care is taken to protect adjacent teeth and tissue. In rare cases, minor superficial damage to enamel near the treated site or to a small adjacent area of gum may occur. Such damage typically heals without further intervention.
  10. Hygiene is critical for healing: Diligent oral hygiene during the healing phase is essential. After 24 hrs, warm saltwater rinses (½ tsp / 8 oz) 2–3×/day. Chlorhexidine 0.12% rinse if prescribed. Gentle soft-toothbrush cleaning of surrounding teeth from day 2.
  11. Tobacco / smoking: Tobacco use slows healing, increases the risk of regrowth, and may darken the healing tissue. The patient agrees to avoid tobacco for at least 72 hours post-procedure (and ideally throughout healing).
  12. Frenectomy specifics (if applicable): A frenectomy releases a tight or low-attached frenum to permit gingival closure or tongue / lip mobility. Mild discomfort and a small white healing area are expected. Speech, eating, and oral function recover within days.
  13. Operculectomy specifics (if applicable): Removal of an operculum (gum flap over a partially-erupted tooth, typically a molar) eliminates a food trap and allows the tooth to erupt normally. The flap can re-form if eruption stalls — a re-procedure may be needed.
  14. No guarantees: As with any procedure, the outcome cannot be guaranteed. The risks above may occur despite proper technique and care.

Informed Consent — Sleep Apnea Oral Appliance
Oral appliance therapy for snoring and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA).

Patient Information

Required

Risks, Benefits & Limitations

Review Verbally
Snoring vs. OSA: Snoring is noise from partial airway closure during sleep — it may be only the noise itself, but it may also indicate a more serious disorder, Obstructive Sleep Apnea. OSA is a serious condition where the airway closes many times during the night, reducing blood oxygen and disrupting sleep. OSA is associated with daytime sleepiness, irregular high blood pressure, and increased risk of heart attack and stroke.

OSA Oral Appliance Acknowledgments

  1. Medical / sleep specialist diagnosis required: Because any sleep-disordered breathing may represent a health risk, all individuals are advised to consult with their physician and / or sleep specialist for an accurate diagnosis before oral appliance therapy.
  2. Candidacy: Oral appliances may be helpful for snoring and sleep apnea. Patients diagnosed with mild or moderate OSA are better candidates for improvement with this therapy than those with severe OSA. Appliances work by protruding the mandible and keeping the tongue forward, opening the airway in the throat.
  3. No guarantees: Clinical research supports oral appliance benefit for many patients, but there are no guarantees this therapy will be successful for every individual. Each person presents unique circumstances. Some patients may be unable to tolerate the appliance.
  4. Side effects: Some patients develop temporary side effects such as excessive salivation, sore jaw joints (TMJ), sore teeth, and slight bite changes. These usually diminish within an hour of appliance removal. On rare occasions a permanent bite change may occur, possibly requiring specialized dental care.
  5. Annual appliance check: The oral appliance should be checked at least once a year for proper fit, with the mouth and jaw joint examined for healthy condition. If unusual symptoms occur, do NOT continue wearing the appliance — schedule an office visit and contact the dentist immediately.
  6. Subjective vs. objective improvement: Patients may feel more refreshed and alert during the day after sleeping with an oral appliance. This subjective evidence may be misleading. The only way to accurately measure whether the appliance keeps oxygen levels sufficient to prevent abnormal heart rhythms is a follow-up sleep test, performed in consultation with the sleep specialist, while wearing the appliance.
  7. Diagnostic records & co-management: Baseline records (models, photos, x-rays, etc.) may be obtained. Consultations with other specialists — sleep specialists, ENT, oral & maxillofacial surgeons — may be required for proper diagnosis and treatment.
  8. Financial responsibility: While the appliance may be covered by medical insurance, the patient accepts financial responsibility for therapy. The patient acknowledges receipt of a copy of this consent form.

Adapted from the American Association of Orthodontists Obstructive Sleep Apnea Informed Consent (2024).
